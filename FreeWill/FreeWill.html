On free will

How trustworthy is our intuition? Our common sense?
How does common sense compare to that which is not common?
To the very fast, to the very slow? To the large and the very small?
To very smart and the very dumb?

To what we choose, and how we choose?

Fate

There was a story about a boy, his name was Oedipus. His father, King Liaus of Thebes, heard a prophecy from an Oracle that his boy was fated to kill his father and marry his mother. Terrified, he left his own child to die in the mountains.
But shepherds picked him up and he eventually was raised as the prince of another city.
When he came of age, Oedipus heard of his prophecy. Terrified, as he did not want to kill his father and marry his mother, he ran away from the city he was raised in, to Thebes.
In his adventures, he encountered his birth-father Liaus and in a quarrel, he killed him.
Then he freed Thebes from the threat of a Sphinx, earning the hand of the Queen of Thebes, his mother. Unbeknown to him, all attempts to escape fate wove it.

Inevitably, what was to be, became.

Determinism

Fate is the precursor to determinism. The idea that one event leads inevitably to another like a chain of dominos, and no matter what we do, we're just along for the ride.

The more we learnt about the world, the more it seemed to follow a cause and effect. Physics tells us that if you know the initial conditions, then you know everything that will follow.
This is determinism.

If the laws of physics describe so accurately the world, allowing us to predict the seasons, the stars, the fundamental makings of the cosmos, how can we ever break free from those laws?
Do we simply fall into the chain of events, no differently than a rock tumbling down a hill?
But then again, why can't science predict our behaviour? Or the weather?
Maybe there's things that science can't explain, and maybe that's where free will lies.

Maybe determinism and free will could coexist?

Maybe science got it wrong and the laws don't work.
Things aren't as clockwork, and magic can happen.
At least sometimes?

The Wound of the Human Ego

In the beginning, there was nothing but me. Or you.
The self.
Like children, we were the center of our own universe.
Children are like tiny little sociopaths, unaware of other people


Eventually, we understand, there are others, with their own minds, with wants and needs.
The rain was a gift from the gods, and stars were signs of times to come.
If we pleased them, the weather would be good to us, and if we displeased them, there could be droughts and storms, but in the end, our actions determined the weather, as we pleased or not the spirits.

Your birth-star could dictate your fate, the sun was a god, and the spirits of the ancients our guides.

We danced and thus rain fell. We sacrificed animals and deluges stopped. Volcanos rested.

All knew that Omphalos was the center of the world. Or maybe Yggdrasil or Mount Olympus, or Uluru?


But we had to build bigger and better temples to the old gods. But as our buildings got bigger, we had to get smarter. We had to keep track of how many sheep were ours, and not our neighbours.
How many sheep we traded to get more sticks and stones, for our buildings.
And when to plant crops, and when to feed them to the goats...
But then, maybe rain was not from the gods.

Ptolemy tracks the motions of the stars, the retrograde motion of Venus, motions of the planets and draws a model of the motion of the cosmos. With the earth at its center.
It predicts the motion of the stars, and other celestial bodies.
And it works just fine.

Copernicus wasn't satisfied. Everything would make so much more sense if the Sun was at the center of the cosmos. Why would such a much smaller object, the Earth, be the center of the universe?
The sun clearly was the center of the universe.

Disaster, from the Greek Dus-Aster, bad star, omens of impending doom in the form of comets had been used by naysayers for centuries.
Except they are rather satellites in our solar system. Halley discovers that one such omen came about every 70 years.
The bad star was no disaster any more.

Newton started predicting with unparalleled precision the movements of celestial bodies. The forces that pushed and pulled the large elements of the cosmos began to be deciphered.

Columbus had tables with predictions of such celestial bodies. He uses this knowledge to trick native Jamaicans into giving him more supplies, or his god would not return the moon.
He knew that the eclipse was coming.
Alas, the Jamaicans did not.

Van Leeuwenhoek noticed these little animals that lived in the water, so tiny we couldn't see them with our limited eyes.

Pasteur and John Snow (he did know something) figure out these little animals can harm us.
They cause disease. Not the curses of witches and shamans.
Not the punishment of the gods.
Just these tiny little things that live everywhere.

Storms and rains stopped being divine punishments and gifts.
Volcanos did not erupt in the rage of the gods.
Earthquakes were not caused by our sins or our distancing from the ways of our ancients.

Darwin's Origin of the Species relates us to apes. Recognizing us as animals and not, in fact, greater than them. We share 50% of our DNA with bananas, and we have more in common with mushrooms still.

The industrial revolution replaces a lot of hand labour, the trades and the guilds start disappearing, man being replaced by machine.

The sun is not the center of the universe anymore. The white streak of milk across the night sky was in fact the center of a huge collection of stars.

It was a galaxy.
A collection of 100'000'000'000 stars. One hundred billion stars.

Each with its own story, just like our own.

And it was only in the 20th century that we realize that some of these dim blobs of light in the sky, where not even stars...
They were whole galaxies in their own right. And there are as many galaxies in this universe of ours, as there are stars in our own common galaxy...

If there were 10 billion people on earth, we'd each get 10 galaxies.
1'000'000'000'000 stars for each one of us.

The human ego was so deeply bruised that it has since not recovered. Not only were we not the center of the world, but we weren't particularly special among animals.
And machines could do our job. We were replaceable and expendable.
Diminute. Ununique. Insignificant.

And while the universe might not have a center, we certainly are at the center of ours.

Anthropocentrism is a difficult filter to get rid of.

Our incredibly tiny perspective of the world around us is a limitation as deeply ingrained as our own DNA.

Your Free Will

So what is "Free Will"?

"Will" is the choice that is made, and "Free" is that it is not coerced, determined only by "you", the agent.

In philosophy, everything has to be well-defined, but we can never agree on the definitions.
So while we may disagree, let me show what to disagree on.

Will

If "Will" is what results in actions, what has "Will"? If we assign "Will" only to humans, we fall prey to anthropocentrism. Some would assign "Will" to most animals, so maybe as far as dogs, cats.
But ants?

How about cockroaches? We've figured out how to turn them left and right at least most of the times.

In nature, there's rarely clear lines, everything exists in a continuum.

A cockroach has around 1 million neurons, whereas humans around 100'000 million neurons. But elephants have about three times that.
On the other hand, some worms have about 300 of them.
Do they have will, when they are over a thousand times simpler than our remote-controlled cockroaches?

How many neurons does it take to will?

Free

Then there's "Free". "Free" from what?
Coercion also exists in a spectrum.

Suppose a person has been raised all their lives to believe one way or act one way, there's hardly any possibility they won't act in some particular way, or at least it makes some of their actions very predictable. Were they coerced? Brainwashed?
Maybe I tell a child he absolutely needs to write with his right hand, because the left hand is that of some evil deceiver. And he was a lefty. But to his dying age, he never wrote with his left hand.

What if I just convince you through some reward, say salary, or the threat of a mild punishment, say a spanking. Is that coercion or convincing? Is the spanking a threat or a reward?
Are you just a kinky individual? Is kinkiness genetic?

What if I just insist a lot? Is 50 no's and 1 yes, still a yes?

If I hold a gun to your head, and ask you to dig your own grave, you could say that's coercion... But you could technically refuse. You could exert your ultimate radical freedom and do nothing, preferring death over bending the knee.

In short, unless I physically insert cathodes to your brain and make you think some way, it's not clear where coercion starts... But technically, I don't have to, we have other techniques of brainwashing and hypnosis to make you do things.

There's a case of a man who became pedophillic due to a brain tumor.
There's a case of a man who's craneal accident changed his personality completely.


And even a mild drug such as Tylenol can affect our decision making.
That's why anti-depressants work in the first place.
What about hormones? Brain injury? Psychoactive drugs? Memory manipulation?
I mean, it's not like something like half of humans experience weird mood swings by no fault of their own due to some silly hormones, that would be ludicrous!

The number of ways our brain can be manipulated is outstanding...

And if everything is constrained to the laws of physics, then the only thing that we can be free from would be these laws, then we're bound to these laws, we are not that much different than rocks rolling down a rocky hill. We're physical things simply reacting as the laws permit.

In other words, if we are not free from the chain of causation, the dominoes from the beginning to the end, if we can't pick whether or not to obey the laws of physics, there's no freedom to be had.

If we're not free from causation, we are not free, because it's the only thing to be free from.

On the other hand, we seemingly cannot chose to make a decision because that's still a decision in of itself.

You (the Agent)

Then there's "You". This related to identity.

Who are you? Your body? Your mind? Your soul? Your brain?
What if I split your brain in two, causing you to have two different brains, with differing opinions in your own head?

One half being an atheist, the other one not?
What if you have three or four personalities in your head?
Who is the "you "making the choosing, if every choice ultimately alters who "you" are?
What if like the Ship of Thessus, you change so much that none of your old you remains?
Are you to be blamed for actions that your old self once committed?

A person you can't even recognize?
What about the self you will one day be?

What if you, suffering from dual personality disorder find yourself stuck in a situation, that your other self put you in? What choice did you have in that?

What if you're under the influence of mind-altering substances, or things that change your decision making?

As it was once said:

"No man steps into the same river twice, for it is not the same river, and it is not the same man."

Who are you? How many are you? What is you?

Determinism vs Predictability

It is often argued that if it were the case that if we're fully determined, we should all behave roughly the same way, and we should all be at least in principle very predictable.

But physics does not predict predictability.
When we discussed knowing the initial conditions we did not discuss what if they were ever so slightly different.
This has to do with what is known as Chaos Theory or the Butterfly Effect.

It does not take a very complicated system for things to get really unpredictable. Something as simple as a double pendulum is incredibly sensitive to initial conditions.

A double pendulum remains unpredictable, but remains a thing that has no freedom from the laws of physics and definitely has no will.

It doesn't matter if you are two twin brothers, or two pendulums stuck together, it doesn't seem that the variety of observed events has anything to do with dismissing determinism or demonstrating free will.

It's very tempting to hide away free will behind a quadruple-pendulum, or quintuple or whatever;
some system so complex it is in practice unpredictable, and hence we hide the "freedom" in things that are just too difficult to model, but are in principle, still, perfectly predictable.

Things can be for all practical purposes unpredictable, yet remain perfectly deterministic

Omniscience vs Determinism

An omniscient being, that knows every decision you'll ever make, like a bearded man in the sky or something, would be a strong indication that determinism is true.

As such, since a bunch of people believe in that being, they often conclude that determinism is true, but also generally think that free will is still true. They have their reasons, but I want to say why I think the two are irrelevant.
Omniscience does not imply determinism

If history is a particular path on a plane of all possible paths, then omniscience is seeing the plane, not just the path. Seeing the plane, and knowing all possible outcomes.
You could then say that if he knows the path, then the path is predetermined. Still, we know all the path going backwards into our own history and it doesn't seem more or less predetermined.
To a higher dimensional being, the past and the future could be indistinguishable.
Whether or not seeing time as a line, or all possible times as a plane is even possible in theory or how that would be, I don't know.

You know the decisions made in the past, that knowledge does not necessarily mean that the past was deterministic.
You also can very easily predict the decisions that will be made by your best friend in a given situation, and while that hints to determinism, it doesn't necessarily mean it's deterministic.

The point is there's no reason why omniscience (or knowledge) should necessarily imply determinism.

Artificial Intelligence vs Free Will

Do planes fly? Think about it, what does it mean to fly exactly?
Saying that artificial intelligence doesn't think, is like saying planes don't fly because they don't flap their wings.

Not convinced? I wouldn't have been convinced when Deep Blue beat the reigning Chess master in 1997.
Hell, I wasn't convinced when Watson beat humans at Jeopardy, or when Alpha Go beat the world champion of a game that makes chess looks like tic-tac-toe .

Or when OpenAI beat arguably the best DOTA player.

In a nutshell, this level of AI is not crazy complicated to understand, the AI tries stuff and learns from what the results are. You expose it to new stuff and it uses what it has learnt to make a modified choice.

Now, thinkers in the past didn't have to dwell in this question, but now we do. How do we know if artificial agents can have will, and whether it is free or not?

The problem here begins in the fact that sufficiently sophisticated AI would be indistinguishable from human behaviour. That might seem like an episode of Black Mirror, but telling someone in 1970's that in 40 years time they'd be capable of accessing piles of information in a tiny computer in their pocket would too.
Hell, the wright brothers thought they'd never achieve flight during their lifetime, and there are many such bad predictions about tech, both pessimistic and optimistic. But here it is not a question of whether that level of Ai will be achieved in this life-time, but just the thought that it is possible.

What is experience for a human, it's data to a machine, DNA is code, and stimuli are input.
Once we modeled something like or better than a human brain, we have to ask the question whether a machine is capable of will, and whether that will is free.

While in science the concern is with the when, in philosophy it is the if.

If it is indeed possible, then what would make these machines any less special than us?

That they were designed by humans? Arguably, we were designed by nature, in a process of trial and failure we call evolution...
That they are machines? Arguably, we are biological machines...
That we impose our will on them? Arguably, it needn't be the case that we tell it what to do, and arguably, the will of nature is imposed on us.
That it is not free? Arguably, they can use the same physical processes we use, if not more...

It becomes hard to argue that humans are capable of free will, but not sufficiently sophisticated machines.
If there's no critical distinction between the two, then both are either capable or incapable of free will.

What People Think

People have a wide range of opinions on the matter, and this topic pulls, as quickly discussed, on all corners of ideas, from science, religion, technology to nature, identity and definitions.

There's instincts and genes, and there's that which is taught to us, Nature vs. Nurture.
This debate has raged for a long time. And this too plays in this dilemma.

It can be argued that all of these influences our decision to some extent, so we could have partial free will.
The question then becomes to what extent.

Some believe that free will also exists in a continuum, some believe in one of four big choices.

Fig. a: Some cute image on the web on different stances

Hard Determinism

Determinism is true (spoilers, it isn't), and so all of our actions are determined, actually pre-determined since the beginning of time.
There's no free will, we're dominoes riding the passage of events.
You had no choice but to take the bus this morning because you were going to do that silly job at the super-market from the moment you were born into that ill-fortunate middle-class family that can't afford to pay for your schooling. Sure, you could have stuck to trade-school and be done, but you were ambitious.
You were always ambitious.

You were born ambitious, which was nurtured by the fire in your mother's heart and the charisma of your father, the thatcher who taught you you could change your stars.

Soft Determinism (Compatibilism)

So, what if everything was already decided? You are your will. The thing that makes you "you", made the decision and no one forced you to take that bus. No one put a gun to your head and coerced you.

You are the agent of your will, if you are free to do as you will.
You took the bus because you chose. And that's what matters!

Compatibilism argues that the only meaningful way to talk about you, freedom and will, is within the context of what is, rather than what could be.
You are a brain, within which processes that are not well understood occur and from which choices happen.
This choices may be influenced by quantum physics and are thus "free" from causality.
It's the only fair way in a world without magic to discuss "Free Will"

Libertarianism

Science can't even predict if it's going to rain tomorrow. It's a useful tool for doing some stuff, like creating a computer in which we run sophisticated physics simulations, but it's limited.
Hell, science can't even get quantum physics right, and quantum physics predicts the world is not deterministic. Random shit can happen, including us making decisions. Sure, you might not chose how tall you'll be, but you definitely can chose what you do.

But at a core, we are free. More or less free depending on say, how drunk we are, or how we were raised, but for the most part, we can create our own chain of events.

Sure, if the world was deterministic, we'd have no free will, but since we have free will (as it seems we do) then determinism is false!

We are not only dominoes, we are domino-chain makers, not just observers.

We weave our own fate, forge our own destiny!

Hard Incompatibilism

If the bus does not show up, can you choose to take the bus? What if the bus both shows up and doesn't?
Can you both chose to take the bus or walk, as the bus shows up, or doesn't?

There's too much to be said about the indeterministic nature of the world, but we don't have the time (or chose not to?).

Simply put, very very tiny things behave probabilistically. There's a thing that's most likely to happen, but it might not. Something else might happen.
The best we can do is predict the odds of an event happening.

Now, at this point, some people argue ardently one of two things:

  • There it is! Our free will. A quantum consciousness capable of transcending the simple laws of physics.
  • God does not play with dice. Things aren't just probabilistic or random. Science simply does not understand the deeper underlying hidden laws. Things are still deterministic in some higher level.

The first one leads us back to Libertarianism, and the second one back to Determinism.

Let's talk about the first point, indeterminism.

Sigh... Here we hit the limits of human understanding (and mine).
To start with the first point, (our current mainstream interpretation of) quantum mechanics remains probabilistic, which means 90% of the time or more it will land exactly where you expect it to land. So, we can at most be expected to be some percentage "free".

And that's assuming that quantum physics actually impacts our "consciousness". We can explain a vast amount of phenomena in the world without invoking the probabilistic quantum nature.

It's only when we make transistors so small that quantum tunneling becomes a problem, or when we make two tiny slits and send individual particles into it. While our brains have lots of small cells, they're not nearly small enough for quantum weirdness to kick in.
So even if indeterminism gives us the opportunity to create causal chains, we probably don't.
But suppose we do. Suppose 10% of the decisions you make, at random, you instead roll a dice, and based on the outcome, you do something.

You make a table with choices, roll a dice, and based on the number, you do the choice in the row the dice rolled.

And then you say, "Behold! My freedom!"…

Then you remember the same happens to tape-worms. And actually, sub-atomic particles in your coffee.
So, while coffee might not be considered an "agent" or have "will", it has the same kind of "freedom" you're invoking to be "free". And the tape-worm has it all.

Hell, a quantum random number generator does just the trick, I'll lend it to you at the next fork in the road you cross.

That is to say, you'd have to consider a dice-roll (on some small percent of the decisions you make) to be your free will, assume that percentage is even significant, and assume that there's indeed a dice-roll in the first place. And worms do it too.

Now back the second point, it simply says determinism is still true, we're just too stupid to get it.
We don't yet understand the "hidden causes" behind the dice-roll-looking behaviour. 

Einstein spent the latter part of his life defying quantum physics... He never came to terms with the fact that reality is inherently random and probabilistic.

The idea that there's something else determining things, and we just don't know it yet is the idea of Hidden Variable Theory.
Since it doesn't change of free will discussion, we'll just leave that little nugget for the sake of completeness and as an exercise for the reader.

Whether or not physical laws are deterministic, remains to be seen. It's probable that as the scientific knowledge gets refined, we sway from one end to another. Either way, if your idea of "free will" requires freedom from causation you could end up disappointed, so I would not count on it.

What I Think

Thanks for asking.
It appears to me, as stated not too subtly in this totally impartial piece, that we like to feel special, but we aren't.
Will is not well-defined, but also assigned arbitrarily to some things and not others.
Randomness does not constitute freedom, coercion exists in a spectrum, so we can't rely on arbitrary notions of "free from coercion".
The Ego is constantly changing, possibly multiple such egos, so the agent is ill-defined.

We keep looking for answers, and find none...

The Question

So, instead of collecting answers, let's collect questions:

  • What's capable of Will? Humans? Dogs? Ants? Bacteria? Mitochondria? Viruses? Prions?
    • Where do you draw the line, and why?
    • If you draw it at humans, when did humans acquire will? At Homo Sapiens? Homo sensorium? Homo Erectus? Australopithecus?
      • Where do you draw that line and why?
  • Are only living beings capable of Will?
    • What is a living being ? Are viruses technically alive? How about computer parasites that exhibit more characteristics of being alive than viruses? Why are viruses not considered living? Why aren't computer parasites?
    • Why are living beings so special that they should have this thing Will and some less living but equally awesome and dynamic phenomena do not ?
  • What does it mean to be free? Free from what?
    • Is freedom from causation required for free will?
    • Is coercion a black or white matter?
    • Where does radical freedom come in? Do we not always have a choice?

To draw an absolute line anywhere requires some very heavy arguments, particularly because these are all spectrums and the boundaries are more than fuzzy. What is it so special of a free-willing entity that other entities do not have?

You can argue that you don't need to identify a clear division, which could be fair, but it could also indicate that you're talking about a useful metaphor, rather than a concrete thing at all.

It appears to me that Will is a word to explain a complicated idea, like feelings, but it does not map to physical phenomena directly.

Freedom, or liberty has no better luck. Without quantum physics, we can't create new causal chains, so first we have to assume our brain is affected by quantum level noise.
And then, that's the same noise we get in empty space. It's not a characteristic of the being, just inherent noise in the physical world.

In order to show free will, you'd have to be able to untangle the problem of freedom, the problem of will and the problem of self.
All of them.

The Game (you just lost it)

The laws of physics aren't rules that constraint what we can do.
They are rules that drive it.

So, if we have rules, let's make a game. Let's also pretend that the game is not in our world cause then we get into a free-will-recursion or something... since wouldn't using something of this world as a metaphor that itself would behave under the rules of the world be self-contradictory? (was the game determined to be that way? Are the cards destined to be shuffled that way? A dream within a dream? Azathoth? Is Inception a good movie? Where are my pills?).

To Drink or Not To Drink

Let's say I shuffle a deck of cards, and then me and my friends draw one card at a time. We look at the card and based on the card, we must follow some rule, like let's say five means drinks for guys, six for chicks, three for me, two for you and whatever.

Now, if the rules don't permit me to do anything, the cards that will be drawn were determined the moment they were shuffled.
That doesn't make it predictable, as there are in fact more ways to shuffle a deck than there are atoms on Earth, and someone should tell that to my drunk friend since I keep drawing three.

If you peeked at the deck though, then you would know every single card that would be drawn.

Sure, King's Cups rules require some decisions to be made AFTER, but that needn't be the case... You might drink once per face value of the card for example, and the cards you will draw have all been determined before you start.

This is like hard-determinism, the rules are set, the future is set and we're just getting shit faced.

Back to Square One

Now suppose instead we play snakes and ladders. In this case, we take turns rolling the dice and then we see what happens.
No one knows how it's going to happen, except maybe if the computer is simulating the dice-roll, in which case the fact that IT knows, doesn't mean that it makes any difference for the players. In my hypothetical drinking game, all the information about what's gonna happen in the game is already in the game.

We don't exert any decision (besides playing a really pointless game).
We don't get to skip our turn, or roll 1 die instead of 2 or whatever, we're just rolling a dice and acting on it.

Just like real life... err I mean hard indeterminism.

Tic Tac Nope

Tic Tac Toe is a game so simple that every simple possibility fits in a piece of paper. We have decisions, but not really meaningful in the big picture.

You either play perfectly and draw, or don't play perfectly and probably still draw.

This is closer to Soft Determinism, the idea that you kinda-sorta have a choice, but it's effectively been decided because you really can't pick the corner square unless you're stupid.

How Deep is your Will?

If we picked something like chess though, we have some restrictions of what we can do by the rules, but the amount of combinations is so ridiculously high, that you may as well consider it as if it was free, and requires will. Sure, you can't move the pawn like a queen, but you can chose which pawn to move.

The player has to be outside the game, he's playing the knight, he's not the knight being played...

But, if somehow, the thing playing us, which must be outside the game, is also us; like a soul or some other magic that exists outside the physical world and its rules, then sure! Free-will away!

Either way, this game is the closest we get to an analogy for libertarianism, everything is possible and you get to choose. The crown jewel of human intellect: Chess.

I mean, of course, except Deep Blue beat the chess master in 1996 so... That's how deep that ocean goes...

Existential Angst Triggered

So what do we do about it?

In some sense, the problem of free will is like the problem of Jupiter pulling you out of bed in the morning with its gravitational force.
It is both true and mostly doesn't matter.
No, you don't have free will, but you have no choice to live as if you did.
But there are three big takeaways you should try to get from this:

  • Don't praise yourself too much. You're lucky for any of your achievements, whether because you rolled well on the genetic dice, on the country dice, on the nurture dice, on the quantum-decision dice.
  • Don't be too hard on others. You have no idea of the chain of dominoes weighing on the back of that person, forcing them (or pushing them, or coercing them, whatever) to make the decision they made.

Now, there are some pretty big ethical implications of free-will, like why punish someone who's got no free-will, and that comes back to Jupiter.

  • Be practical. So what if you are a biological automaton worrying about how a Gamma-Ray Burst might wipe half of the earth's life at any moment. You still have to have breakfast and feed your spawnlings. Just don't take it too seriously.

In a sense, trying to explain free will is like trying to explain qualia, like explaining to a bat what orange looks like, and much like color, the firmament, consciousness, borders, spontaneous generation, civilization and the soul, they're really just to abstract complex phenomena, pretty metaphors that make it easier to navigate the world.
The Sun does not rise in the East, we spin commulatively in circles to meet it.
To talk about them in simple words makes life easier, even though they aren't really real.

As far as you measly mortal is concerned, in this large and indifferent cosmos, there's no choice, but to make a choice.
When you watch the ant scurry about it's business, it doesn't worry about your great power and understanding, and neither should you worry about the universe.
Sure, this choice is an illusion elaborated by your brain, like your sense of self and your weird dislike of Nickelback.

You can't chose to not chose, since that's a choice, even if that choice is illusory. You still in charge of your destiny.

I embrace the absurdity of acting like I have a choice, because I have no other choice.
